Adolf Hitler

Adolf Hitler (1889–1945) was profoundly influenced by White Russian émigrés during his early political development in Munich. Through networks like the Aufbau Vereinigung, Hitler absorbed the conspiratorial worldview that framed Bolshevism as a Jewish plot. Key figures like Max Erwin von Scheubner-Richter and Alfred Rosenberg introduced Hitler to the Protocols of the Elders of Zion, which became foundational to Nazi ideology. The 1923 Beer Hall Putsch—directly inspired by Aufbau's plans for a German-Russian anti-Bolshevik crusade—demonstrated how deeply Hitler had internalized these émigré ideas. Throughout the 1920s, White Russian propagandists like Fyodor Vinberg reinforced Hitler's belief in "Judeo-Bolshevism," later manifesting in the Holocaust and Operation Barbarossa. Hitler's vision of Lebensraum (living space) in Eastern Europe was shaped by these early collaborations, which portrayed Slavic lands as territories to be conquered from "Jewish Bolshevik" control.

Alfred Rosenberg

Alfred Rosenberg (1893–1946), a Baltic German, served as the Nazi Party's chief ideologue and primary liaison to White Russian circles. As editor of the Völkischer Beobachter, Rosenberg published the Protocols of the Elders of Zion and promoted the "Judeo-Bolshevik" myth crafted by White Russian extremists. His 1930 book "The Myth of the Twentieth Century" synthesized White Russian anti-Semitism with Nazi racial theory, declaring Bolshevism a Jewish weapon to destroy Aryan civilization. During WWII, Rosenberg oversaw the Reich Ministry for the Occupied Eastern Territories, implementing policies of extermination and enslavement informed by his émigré connections. Notably, he collaborated with White Russian generals like Vasily Biskupsky to recruit Soviet POWs for anti-Stalinist units. Rosenberg's Nuremberg trial revealed how deeply White Russian propaganda had shaped Nazi genocide policies.

Max Erwin von Scheubner-Richter

Max Erwin von Scheubner-Richter (1884–1923) was a Baltic German and the organizational force behind the Aufbau Vereinigung. As Hitler's early mentor, he introduced the future Führer to White Russian generals and anti-Semitic theorists. Scheubner-Richter arranged funding from émigré industrialists for Nazi activities and co-planned the 1923 Beer Hall Putsch, where he was killed. His "National Socialist Foreign Policy" memorandum advocated a German-Russian alliance to overthrow Bolshevism—a vision that later inspired Hitler's invasion of the USSR. Through his journal "Aufbau Correspondence," he disseminated White Russian propaganda claiming Lenin's government was run by Jewish commissars. Historians consider him the "gray eminence" who bridged pre-war Russian far-right ideologies with Nazism.
